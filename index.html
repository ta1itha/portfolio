The Stack

Some people follow a straight path. I've been pushing commits.

Developer → educator of 700+ students → product manager → business analyst. Each version shipped new thinking. Each role taught me how users, systems, and businesses actually work from the inside out.

I started in engineering, where I shipped iOS, Android, and web features at a fintech startup and abstracted 90% of duplicated code into a reusable package at a legal-tech firm. Then I spent three years teaching front-end development, running Scrum ceremonies for student teams, and iterating on the LMS itself.

Now I sit at the intersection I was always moving towards: technical enough to talk to engineers, empathetic enough to understand users, and analytical enough to build a business case from first principles.

I also have a strong conviction: if you're not doing anything with AI, you're choosing to be slower. In my current role as Student Success Manager, I'm actively using PowerAutomate to automate manual onboarding and administrative workflows — because time spent on repetitive process is time stolen from work that actually matters. I believe every administrative task that can be automated should be automated, and I won't wait to be asked.

Product Management Internship

Product Strategy · Agile · Process Improvement

The Product Was the Internship Itself

Most interns execute someone else's roadmap. At Stroop Digital, the internship is the product and you are the PM. The mandate: follow a structured agile learning pathway, ship real PM deliverables, and apply product strategy to improve the internship process for every cohort that comes after you. For a developer who has spent years refactoring other people's inefficient systems, this was a natural fit.

→ Self-managed delivery owned the full PM lifecycle end-to-end: discovery, prioritisation, roadmap, execution, and retrospective with no one assigning tickets.
→ Learning pathway as backlog treated each module (Business Canvas, OKRs, Customer Journey, MVP Design) as a sprint deliverable, tracking progress and blockers independently.
→ Process improvement output applied product strategy to identify friction points in the internship experience and proposed improvements to the programme for future interns.
→ Developer instinct, PM lens brought a builder's bias for optimisation: if something was slow, unclear, or repeated I documented it, reasoned about it, and proposed a better version.

The Setup

Stroop Digital Academy runs a structured Agile Product Manager programme where the intern owns the entire experience: there's no manager handing you a brief. You're given a learning pathway and told to ship. The modules Business Canvas, Product Canvas, Customer Journey mapping, OKRs, MVP Design & Roadmap, Product Backlog, Delivery, Value Measurement aren't just theory. Each one is a deliverable you produce, review, and iterate on.

The Challenge

The actual product you're building is the internship itself. You're expected to apply everything you learn to identify what works, what doesn't, and how to make the programme better for the cohort behind you. That means thinking like a PM from day one, not just completing tasks, but asking why the tasks exist, whether they're the right ones, and what a better version looks like.

Why It Clicked

As a developer, I've always been wired to look at a system and ask: where's the waste? Stroop gave me a framework to channel that instinct productively. Every friction point became a user story. Every repeated confusion became a process gap worth closing. I wasn't just learning product management; I was practising it on the thing in front of me.
